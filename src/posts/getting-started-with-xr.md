# Embracing the Ephemeral: How to Get the Most out of a Hackathon

> **No generative AI was used to write this post.**

On the morning of November 11, 2024, I was waiting in a security line at the San Francisco Airport, when an older man behind me struck up a conversation regarding the hoodie I was wearing. It was a fleecy freebie I got for attending Hack the North 2024 with the text "Waterloo Hack Week" on it. 

It turned out the man was a Waterloo alum from thirty-something years ago, and was fascinated by the concept of "hacking" being a competition. I explained how grueling hackathons were, and how I was just flying back home from an intense hacking weekend myself. After a few minutes, it was time for me to take my liquids and laptops out of my bag and proceed through security.

While going through, it suddenly dawned on me that this man and I had been talking about two completely different things. Based on what I had just explained to him, he was probably picturing groups legitimately trying to *hack* their way into critical software infrastructure. I looked around to see if I could clarify what a hackathon really was (a software building competition) but he was gone, likely assuming he had just met someone who broke into digital systems for fun.

Reflecting on this experience now, it makes me realize how bad of a term *hackathon* actually is. Barring the meaning of the word "hack" traditionally referring to cybercrime, the second part of the word comes from "marathon". Hackathons tend to be far more like sprints, which couldn't be more different from marathons.

It took me embarrasingly long to realize this apparent contradiction. It certainly didn't happen at my first hackathon: a local competition in 2022 called Code the Change that I took part in on the urging of a friend. I'd love to say that we won the competition, and that's what sparked the flame that would lead me to compete obsessively in every hackathon I could find for the next few years. But we didn't win. Perhaps then I'd love to say that despite not winning, I was proud of what my team and I developed at our first hackathon and at least I got to apply my skills to make something cool. But [the project](https://youtu.be/34M_vPXtoVU) genuinely **sucked**. Perhaps then, I'd love to say that presenting the project was a great experience to get feedback and hopefully come back stronger next time. But we didn't even submit - so there was no judging or feedback. It seemed that everything that probably could go wrong, did go wrong.

The first fault came a few hours into our planning - we split up and decided to work on different parts of the application totally separate, and left about an hour on the second day to integrate. Two of our team members ran into car troubles on their way on day two, so they were relegated to hearing about the project's meltdown over text messages. In the end, we couldn't find a way to integrate the backend (a literal Jupyter notebook) with any sort of frontend. By the time we gave up and decided to just submit it, we found out our submission required a YouTube video demo, which we couldn't get uploaded in time.

There was no prize, no feedback, no new connections made, and from the perspective of the hackathon's submission system - no project at all. Looking back at it now, the failure had not been a grand nuclear meltdown, as that would suggest that at some point that turbines were turning and steam was steaming. This project's collapse had been a whimper.

Some of the members from that team (including the person who had initially recruited me) would never do another hackathon again, at least to my knowledge. Everyone moved on - to coursework, other projects, or the ever-present job search.

I didn't move on. I went home and caught up on the rest of the sleep I'd missed that weekend, but it always bothered me how that hackathon played out. I was a second year software engineering student, and I was embarrassed that the best I could do was an unsubmitted Jupyter notebook that was effectively just a Google search wrapper. My motivation to remake the project properly completely died when, a few months later, this thing called ChatGPT became all the rage, and replaced the need for that project idea entirely.

I started with the story of my first hackathon because it tells a tale of someone who got almost nothing out of a hackathon. I didn't get to learn new skills, I didn't go out of my way to meet people, and I didn't get any formalized feedback on how I could improve. The only thing the hackathon did for me was annoy me, and that annoyance would eventually motivate me to take part in every hackathon I could find - but even that came more as a result of realizing my own incompetence than the hackathon itself.

But back in 2022, it was a lot harder to find hackathons than it is now, and it was even harder to convince my friends with technical experience to compete in them with me. I'd have to wait another four months before my next hackathon - a project where my team made a simple [project improving emergency room wait times](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/zaid-ahmed-swe_team-hackathon-medical-activity-7032499517455290368-RFUv?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADjnN3sB4j_wI-A8CirO1PXiijedHBLVMHQ) that would achieve third place. That would kick off a win streak of three hackathons, followed by a losing streak of far more, a few more wins, running Calgary's largest 36-hour hackathon, and finally, this blog post. 

Through each win or loss, I learned something about hackathons, and more importantly, myself. As a developer, engineer, entrepreneur, and advocate. This post is not about how to win hackathons - to be honest, winning was not always my chief goal. I firmly believe you can still make the most out of a hackathon without going home with a prize, and I'll be detailing my opinion on how to achieve that in the rest of this post. 

## Before the Hack

### Defining Invidual Goals

There are lots of reasons for attending a hackathon, and it's important to decide why you're going - whether you want gold and glory or just some free food, it's important to define a high-level goal. The best hackathons will have a lot going on: companies to network with, people to meet, projects to work on, but maybe even llamas to pet and Squid Game challenges to play (Treehacks 2025 had a lot of stress-busters). 

You simply cannot have it all. If you want to win at a competitive hackathon, you'll need to give most of your time to the actual project itself, which may not enable a ton of fun side-encounters. If you want to network with people at the career booths, you'll likely have to sacrifice some time working on your project. Of course, you can balance different priorities over the course of the weekend, but you're likely to leave the weekend unsatisfied if you wanted to have the most fun time, the biggest prize, and the most LinkedIn connections managed. It's worth mentioning that I am familiar with the all-nighter approach to a hackathon - maximizing fun and networking during the scheduled daily hours, and then working through the night. Anyone who's actually done this at a hackathon would probably advise against this, since there is such a thing as being too tired to think. When the project breaks and needs a hotfix, the well-slept hacker is less likely to snap. And the next day, when judges come around, the lively presenter is likely to take home the prize.
